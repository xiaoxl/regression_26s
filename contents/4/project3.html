14  Example: Boston Housing dataset full walkthrough

In the previous section we talk about our main tools to handle the Boston dataset. In this section we display how to explore the dataset from scratch.

Variable Description Type
crim Per capita crime rate by town Numerical, continuous
zn Proportion of residential land zoned for lots over 25,000 sq.ft. Numerical, continuous
indus Proportion of non-retail business acres per town Numerical, continuous
chas Charles River dummy variable (1 if tract bounds river; 0 otherwise) Categorical, nominal
nox Nitrogen oxides concentration (parts per 10 million) Numerical, continuous
rm Average number of rooms per dwelling Numerical, continuous
age Proportion of owner-occupied units built prior to 1940 Numerical, continuous
dis Weighted mean distance to five Boston employment centers Numerical, continuous
rad Index of accessibility to radial highways (larger = better accessibility) Categorical, ordinal
tax Full-value property-tax rate per $10,000 Numerical, continuous
ptratio Pupil–teacher ratio by town Numerical, continuous
black \(1000(B_k - 0.63)^2\), where \(B_k\) is the proportion of Black residents Numerical, continuous
lstat Percentage of lower-status population Numerical, continuous
medv Median value of owner-occupied homes (in $1,000s) Numerical, continuous

library(ggcorrplot)
ggcorrplot(cor_matrix, hc.order = TRUE)

What I’m looking for:

  • Skewed variables (may need log transforms — medv and crim are classically right-skewed)
  • Non-linear relationships between predictors and medv
  • High pairwise correlations between predictors (multicollinearity warning signs)
  • Outliers or influential observations

Here are some key takeaways from the observations:

  • lstat vs medv: curved, which indicates quadratic or log transform likely needed
  • crim is extremely right-skewed, which indicates log(crim) often works better
  • rm vs medv: mostly linear but with some spread
  • nox, dis, age are highly intercorrelated (all related to urban density)
  • tax and rad are nearly collinear

Why: This gives me the lay of the land. I check:

  • Adjusted \(R^2\) (baseline: ~0.73 for raw Boston)
  • Which predictors are significant in the presence of all others
  • Residual standard error
  • Overall F-test

Then immediately do residual diagnostics on this full model:

par(mfrow = c(2, 2))
plot(fit_full)

The four diagnostic plots give me:

  1. Residuals vs Fitted — checks linearity and homoscedasticity
  2. Normal Q-Q — checks normality of residuals
  3. Scale-Location — another view of heteroscedasticity
  4. Residuals vs Leverage — identifies influential points (Cook’s distance)

On raw Boston data, these plots typically show a fan shape (heteroscedasticity) and non-linearity — which tells me transformations are needed before I proceed.

14.3 Phase 3: Consider Transformations

14.3.1 Check if log(medv) improves things

I’d also use Box-Cox to formally choose the best power transformation for medv:

library(MASS)
boxcox(fit_full)

Reading this specific plot

  • The peak appears to be right around \(\lambda\approx0\), and
  • the 95% CI dotted lines straddle 0 closely. They look to be roughly \((-0.1, 0.2)\) or so.

By the standard rule, if \(\lambda=0\) is inside the 95% CI, log(y) is the transformation.

fit_log <- lm(log(medv) ~ ., data = Boston)
plot(fit_log)
summary(fit_log)
