D.4 Historical Development of \(C_p\), AIC, and Optimism

NoteBig Picture

Modern model selection criteria such as \(C_p\) and AIC can be understood as training error plus a correction for optimism. This interpretation, however, emerged only after their original development.

D.4.1 1. Early Criteria (1970s)

Two fundamental model selection criteria were introduced independently:

  • Mallows’ \(C_p\) (Mallows, 1973)
  • Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) (Akaike, 1973)

D.4.1.1 Mallows’ \(C_p\)

Mallows proposed: \[ C_p = RSS + 2p\sigma^2, \] motivated as an approximately unbiased estimator of prediction error in linear models.

D.4.1.2 Akaike’s AIC

Akaike proposed: \[ \text{AIC} = -2\log L + 2k, \] derived from minimizing the expected Kullback–Leibler divergence between the true model and a candidate model.

WarningImportant

At this stage, \(C_p\) and AIC were derived from different principles:

  • \(C_p\): unbiased estimation of squared prediction error
  • AIC: information-theoretic (KL divergence)

There was no unified framework explaining why both take the form: \[ \text{fit} + \text{penalty}. \]

D.4.2 2. Efron’s Unification (1986)

A major conceptual breakthrough came from Efron (1986), who introduced the concept of optimism.

D.4.2.1 Definition (Optimism)

\[ \text{Optimism} = E[\text{Test Error} - \text{Training Error}]. \]

D.4.2.2 General Result

Efron showed: \[ \text{Optimism} = 2 \sum_{i=1}^n \operatorname{Cov}(\hat{y}_i, y_i). \]

D.4.2.3 Special Cases

  • Linear models: \[ \text{Optimism} = 2p\sigma^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad C_p \]

  • Likelihood-based models: \[ \text{Optimism} = 2k \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{AIC} \]

ImportantKey Insight

Both \(C_p\) and AIC can be written as: \[ \boxed{ \text{Training Error} + \text{Optimism} } \]

D.4.3 3. ESL Framework (2000s)

In The Elements of Statistical Learning, Hastie, Tibshirani, and Friedman systematically presented model selection using the optimism framework.

They showed:

\[ \text{Optimism} = 2\sigma^2 \cdot \text{df}, \]

where \(\text{df}\) (degrees of freedom) generalizes model complexity.

D.4.4 4. Conceptual Summary

TipHistorical Flow
  1. Mallows (1973): \(C_p\) (prediction error perspective)
  2. Akaike (1973): AIC (information-theoretic perspective)
  3. Efron (1986): optimism (unified explanation)
  4. ESL (2000s): modern teaching framework

D.4.5 5. Final Interpretation

\[ \boxed{ \text{Model Selection Criteria} = \text{Training Error} + \text{Bias Correction} } \]

where the bias correction is the optimism, i.e., the expected amount by which training error underestimates test error.

D.4.6 References

  • Mallows, C. L. (1973). Some Comments on \(C_p\). Technometrics.
  • Akaike, H. (1973). Information Theory and an Extension of the Maximum Likelihood Principle.
  • Efron, B. (1986). How Biased is the Apparent Error Rate of a Prediction Rule?
  • Hastie, T., Tibshirani, R., & Friedman, J. (2009). The Elements of Statistical Learning.

D.4.7 6. Other Notable Contributors

Beyond Mallows, Akaike, and Efron, several researchers played key roles in shaping modern model selection theory.

D.4.7.1 🔹 Hirotugu Akaike → Bayesian extension (BIC precursor)

Although AIC is frequentist, it inspired:

  • Schwarz (1978): Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC)

\[ \text{BIC} = -2\log L + k \log n \]

  • Derived from Bayesian model selection
  • Stronger penalty than AIC
  • Consistent (selects the true model under assumptions)

D.4.7.2 🔹 Gideon Schwarz (1978)

  • Introduced BIC
  • Connected model selection with Bayesian marginal likelihood

D.4.7.3 🔹 Sugiura (1978) → AICc

  • Introduced AICc (corrected AIC)

\[ \text{AICc} = \text{AIC} + \frac{2k(k+1)}{n-k-1} \]

  • Fixes small-sample bias in AIC
  • Important when \(n\) is not much larger than \(k\)

D.4.7.4 🔹 Allen (1974) → PRESS / Cross-Validation

  • Introduced PRESS statistic
  • Early form of leave-one-out cross-validation (LOOCV)

\[ \text{PRESS} = \sum (y_i - \hat{y}_{(i)})^2 \]

  • Provides a direct estimate of test error
  • No analytic correction needed

D.4.7.5 🔹 Stone (1977)

  • Showed:

\[ \boxed{ \text{AIC} \;\approx\; \text{LOOCV} } \]

👉 Major bridge between: - analytic criteria (AIC) - resampling methods (CV)

D.4.7.6 🔹 Ye (1998) → Generalized Degrees of Freedom

  • Extended Efron’s idea to nonlinear models

\[ \text{df} = \sum \frac{\partial \hat{y}_i}{\partial y_i} \]

  • Applies to:
    • ridge
    • smoothing splines
    • other adaptive procedures

D.4.7.7 🔹 Tibshirani & Taylor (2012)

  • Degrees of freedom for lasso
  • Extended optimism framework to modern regularization

D.4.8 7. Expanded Conceptual View

TipUnified Perspective

All major methods can be viewed as estimating prediction error:

  • \(C_p\), AIC → analytic bias correction
  • BIC → Bayesian approximation
  • CV / PRESS → direct estimation
  • Modern df → generalize optimism

D.4.9 8. Final Takeaway

\[ \boxed{ \text{Model selection evolved from formulas to a general theory of prediction error estimation} } \]
